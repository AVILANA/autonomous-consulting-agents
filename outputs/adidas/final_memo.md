## 1. WHY NOW

Adidas just closed its best financial year in over a decade. Revenue hit a record €24.8 billion, operating profit grew 54%, and the CEO's contract was extended to 2030. The turnaround is declared complete.

That is exactly why the next chapter is harder — and why the window to engage is now.

The first phase of recovery was won on brand heat (the Samba and Gazelle lifestyle resurgence) and channel repair (re-engaging wholesale partners that the previous management had abandoned). Those are not repeatable levers. Getting from today's 8.3% operating margin to the publicly committed target of more than 10% by 2028 requires roughly €700–900 million in additional operating profit. Brand momentum alone will not deliver it. Smarter operations will.

Three forces are converging simultaneously:

**First:** Adidas's multi-year technology overhaul — called TRANS4RM, the replacement of three legacy systems with one unified SAP platform on Amazon Web Services — is approaching the critical window where the AI (Artificial Intelligence) decision layer must be designed before the data foundation locks in. Miss this window and the AI architecture defaults to whatever was built separately by individual teams. Arrive now and the architecture can be co-designed before go-live.

**Second:** A €200 million tariff headwind is embedded in 2026 guidance, with the risk of further escalation from Vietnam and Indonesia — and no public evidence of AI-powered scenario modeling to manage it. Every month without a systematic answer to this question is a month of unmanaged financial exposure at the CFO's desk.

**Third:** The 2026 FIFA (Fédération Internationale de Football Association) World Cup in North America opens in June. adidas is the exclusive sporting goods partner. The host markets — United States, Mexico, Canada — are the exact region where adidas grew only +4% constant currency in 2025 vs. +13% globally. The brand moment is guaranteed. Whether it creates lasting loyalty relationships or disappears without a data trail depends on whether the direct-to-consumer infrastructure is ready.

The consulting opportunity is not to help adidas recover. It is to make the investments adidas has already made start compounding together — before the window closes.

### Secondary Lever: Quality

Quality in this context means decision quality — specifically, the accuracy of demand forecasting, the precision of supply planning, and the reliability of inventory allocation. At adidas's scale (€4.99 billion in inventory), a 5% improvement in forecast accuracy reduces average inventory held by approximately €250 million and eliminates costly markdowns.

The Samba and Gazelle lifestyle cycle that drove the recovery is culturally driven and trend-dependent. Retro footwear cycles historically peak at two to three years. When this cycle fades, adidas's demand planning system must detect the transition early and reduce production commitments before inventory builds. That is a quality-of-decision problem — and it is not yet solved. The tools exist (o9 Solutions, Amazon SageMaker); the forward-looking data inputs do not. ## 4. SUPPLY CHAIN AI OPPORTUNITIES — DEEP DIVE

Adidas's supply chain is a coordination challenge, not a manufacturing challenge. It does not own a single factory. Its 124 external manufacturing partners ship to 60 distribution centers globally. The decisions that matter — what to make, where to route it, how to allocate it, how to price it — are all information and intelligence problems. This is where AI applies most directly.

### SC-1: Procurement and Supplier Management

**The situation today:**
124 manufacturing partners, 92% concentrated in Asia (Vietnam 27%, Indonesia 19%, China 16%). US-bound sourcing from China has been eliminated. Tariff headwind in 2026 guidance: €200 million. Vietnam and Indonesia carry equivalent US tariff risk — a second escalation scenario that does not appear to be systematically modeled. No public evidence of AI-powered supplier performance management or tariff scenario modeling. [ASSUMPTION — confirmed by absence of any public disclosure]

**The opportunity:**
- **Tariff scenario intelligence:** An always-on AI model that continuously calculates the total landed cost (the full cost of a product including manufacturing, freight, tariffs, and customs duties) across all active sourcing combinations as trade policy changes. Replaces reactive manual analysis — which typically takes days — with decision support available in hours.
- **Supplier performance prediction:** Machine learning models that predict which supplier will deliver late, deliver defects, or face capacity constraints — before the problem occurs. Moves the sourcing team from reviewing last month's performance to acting on next month's predicted risks.
- **Multi-tier supply chain risk mapping:** AI-assisted visibility into second-tier dependencies — for example, whether a Vietnamese factory sources its fabrics from Chinese mills that are themselves tariff-exposed. This type of hidden exposure is currently invisible.

**Expected benefit:** HIGH. A Vietnam or Indonesia-specific tariff escalation of 15–20% could add €200–300 million beyond the €200 million already in 2026 guidance. Early warning on sourcing disruptions reduces emergency air freight costs (5–8× ocean freight rates) and prevents inventory shortfalls at peak demand. [ESTIMATED — based on confirmed production shares of Vietnam 27% and Indonesia 19%]

**Key question to validate:** Has adidas modeled the landed cost impact of a Vietnam or Indonesia-specific tariff escalation? How long does a manual tariff scenario analysis take today?

### SC-2: Demand Planning and Supply Planning

**The situation today:**
o9 Solutions is deployed for in-season retail planning and direct-to-consumer demand sensing. Amazon SageMaker is deployed for seasonal demand forecasting. These are strong tools — but they operate on historical sell-in data (what retailers ordered), not real-time sell-through data (what consumers actually bought at retail). The SAP S/4HANA commercial module that would unify these signals is not yet complete as of April 2026. Wholesale, at approximately 60% of revenue, provides the weakest demand signal.

The Samba and Gazelle cycle is the most visible symptom of a deeper structural problem: adidas's current demand planning brain does not read forward-looking consumer signals. When a product goes viral on social media, existing tools detect it weeks later through sell-in orders — after the stockout has already occurred.

**The opportunity:**
- **Social signal demand sensing:** Connect real-time social media trend data (TikTok views, Instagram engagement, Google search trends) into the o9 forecasting model. For a brand driven by cultural moments, this is not a nice-to-have — it is structurally required for a lifestyle-heavy product portfolio. The Databricks data infrastructure already handles this type of external data feed.
- **Wholesale sell-through intelligence:** Machine learning models predicting stockout and overstock risk at the retail partner level — by retailer, region, and product — before the problem appears in sell-in numbers. Enables proactive replenishment conversations with Foot Locker, Dick's Sporting Goods, and JD Sports.
- **Lifestyle and trend cycle early warning:** AI classification of each product franchise by lifecycle stage (emerging / peak / declining) at the regional level. When the model detects a franchise approaching peak, it automatically reduces forward production commitments — protecting gross margin before the cycle turns.

**Expected benefit:** HIGH. At €4.99 billion in inventory, a 5% improvement in forecast accuracy reduces carrying cost by ~€10–15 million annually and meaningfully reduces markdown exposure. One percentage point of gross margin recovered = €248 million in operating profit. [FACT — revenue base; ESTIMATED — leverage calculation]

**Key question to validate:** What is the current demand forecast error rate (MAPE — Mean Absolute Percentage Error) by product category? What percentage of annual markdown cost is directly attributable to forecast inaccuracy?

### SC-5: Outbound Transportation and Last-Mile Delivery

**The situation today:**
adidas.com e-commerce is €4.3 billion (~18% of revenue), growing at +16% on a currency-neutral basis in FY2025. Last-mile delivery is handled entirely through third-party carriers. Free returns are a competitive expectation in footwear e-commerce. As direct-to-consumer grows, every euro of e-commerce revenue costs significantly more to fulfill than an equivalent wholesale pallet delivery.

**The opportunity:**
- **Carrier selection optimization:** AI selects the optimal carrier for each direct-to-consumer shipment based on cost, historical on-time performance, current capacity constraints, and carbon footprint targets. Replaces static routing rules set once and rarely updated.
- **Returns propensity prediction:** Before a customer clicks "buy," AI estimates the probability of a return based on purchase history, product type, and size selection. Surfaces proactive size guidance for high-risk orders — reducing the return rate at the point of origin rather than managing it after the fact.
- **Predictive delivery promise:** Machine learning predicts the delivery date at order confirmation with high accuracy — rather than quoting the carrier's standard window. Reduces customer service volume and improves satisfaction.

**Expected benefit:** MEDIUM. Each 1 percentage point reduction in e-commerce return rate (assuming a ~25% baseline for footwear) eliminates meaningful reverse logistics cost and improves net revenue per order. [ESTIMATED — 25% is an industry benchmark; adidas-specific rate not publicly disclosed]

## 6. BODY OR BRAIN FIRST?

**The answer for adidas is clear: Brain first.**

The physical network — what we call the "body" — is broadly correct and already under active redesign. Manufacturing supply routes are being restructured right now: China-to-US sourcing eliminated, Vietnam and Indonesia absorbing US-bound volume, India being built as a new manufacturing hub over 3–5 years. The leading distribution centers are world-class. The store estate of 1,933 locations is strategically sound.

The "body" is the physical network: the factories, the distribution centers, the shipping lanes, the stores. The "brain" is how adidas operates within that network: its forecasts, its routing decisions, its supplier risk models, its demand sensing — the intelligence layer on top of the physical infrastructure. If adidas can change something without physically moving or closing something, it is a brain problem.

**Why Brain-first is the right posture for adidas, specifically:**

1. **Body changes take years; brain changes take months.** Building India manufacturing capability takes 3–5 years. AI-powered demand sensing can go live in 6–12 months on existing infrastructure. For adidas to hit its 2028 operating margin target, only brain improvements can deliver results in time.

2. **The brain is incomplete in the highest-leverage areas.** Procurement intelligence is likely pre-AI. Demand sensing is backward-looking. Six AI platforms run separately with no shared architecture or unified governance.

3. **The data backbone is arriving.** TRANS4RM completes by 2027. The opportunity now is to define what the brain will do once the data foundation is complete — and to deploy brain capabilities that don't require TRANS4RM completion today.

4. **The CEO will say yes — if framed correctly.** Gulden's language is "simpler operations, fewer manual steps, better decisions." Frame brain initiatives as operational simplification and decision acceleration, not technology programs.

The discipline Gulden has re-established is the foundation of the current gross margin recovery.

**TRANS4RM must complete.** The SAP S/4HANA migration is the data backbone for almost everything else that matters in AI. Supporting completion — even through developer productivity acceleration — is a prerequisite for the full AI value opportunity.

**Supply chain localization is the right strategic response to tariffs.** "China makes for China" is a sensible framework. **On cost structure vs. peers:**
