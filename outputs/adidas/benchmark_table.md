### 1.5 Total Operating Cost Structure — COGS, SG&A, and Where Logistics Sits

> **Critical methodological note:** In athletic footwear and apparel, logistics, warehousing, and fulfillment costs are classified **within COGS** for some companies and **within SG&A / Operating Expenses** for others. This classification difference means SG&A-only comparisons can be deeply misleading — a company with 34% SG&A may classify distribution costs in COGS, making it appear more efficient than a company with 43% SG&A that correctly classifies the same costs in operating expenses. **Total Operating Cost % Revenue (= COGS% + SG&A% + all operating line items, equivalently = 1 – EBIT Margin%)** is the only reliably comparable metric across the peer set.

| **adidas** | FY2025 | **51.0%** | **49.0%** | **~42.7%** | **91.7%** | **8.3%** | **SG&A / Operating Expenses** |
| Nike | FY2025 | 40.6% | 59.4% | 34.7% | **94.1%** | ~5.9% | **COGS (Cost of Sales)** |

**Summary of classification effect:**

| Classification Type | Companies | Effect on SG&A | Effect on COGS |
|--------------------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Logistics/fulfillment in **COGS** | Nike, Lululemon, Deckers, Under Armour | SG&A appears **lower** by ~4–7pp | COGS appears **higher** by ~4–7pp |
| Logistics/fulfillment in **SG&A / OpEx** | adidas, PUMA, On, Anta, ASICS | SG&A appears **higher** by ~4–7pp | COGS appears **lower** by ~4–7pp |

> **The structural implication for adidas:** Nike's reported SG&A of 34.7% appears 8pp more efficient than adidas's 42.7%. But Nike includes logistics costs in COGS while adidas includes them in SG&A. Adjusting for this classification difference reduces the true gap to an estimated **4–5pp** — material, but roughly half the apparent gap. This does not eliminate the efficiency challenge; it reframes it. The true SG&A efficiency gap vs. Nike is approximately 4–5pp of revenue (~€1.0–1.2B at adidas scale), not 8pp. [INFERENCE — estimated logistics reclassification; requires disclosure-level validation]

**Key observations from the adjusted view:**

1. **The adidas-Nike gap is ~4–5pp (not 8pp) on a logistics-adjusted basis.** Nike's reported SG&A looks best-in-class, but this reflects accounting classification more than genuine operational efficiency. True SG&A efficiency difference is real but more modest than raw numbers suggest. [INFERENCE]

2. **adidas, Lululemon, and Nike all converge to ~40–43% logistics-adjusted SG&A.** Deckers is the genuine outlier at ~38–40% adjusted — its operational leverage from Hoka's DTC growth and lean overhead structure is structurally different. [INFERENCE]

5. **On Holding's ~50.3% SG&A is the highest in the peer set — but at 62.8% gross margin, it is the only company that can structurally afford it.** At adidas's 51% gross margin, On's SG&A ratio would produce an operating loss. Channel and gross margin must expand together for adidas to increase SG&A investment without margin dilution. [INFERENCE — analytical framing]

## 4. KEY PURSUIT HYPOTHESES — WHAT THE BENCHMARK TELLS US

**Hypothesis 1: adidas's true SG&A efficiency gap vs. Nike is ~4–5pp, not ~8pp**
[INFERENCE — logistics reclassification estimated; requires filing-level validation]
> Nike includes warehousing and distribution in Cost of Sales; adidas classifies the same costs in Operating Expenses. This creates an apparent SG&A gap of ~8pp that is substantially a classification difference. On a logistics-adjusted basis, the true gap is ~4–5pp of revenue (~€1.0–1.2B). This does not eliminate the efficiency opportunity — but it reframes the diagnosis: adidas's gap is a real but not catastrophic SG&A challenge, not a structural crisis. Discovery question: what is adidas's absolute logistics cost embedded in OpEx, and how does it compare to Nike's logistics cost embedded in COGS?

**Hypothesis 2: Total Operating Cost % is the correct performance benchmark — and ASICS at 85.3% is the nearest credible target**
[OBSERVABLE — ASICS data; INFERENCE — adidas application]
> On Total Op Cost %, adidas at 91.7% lags ASICS (85.3%), On (87.5%), Deckers (76.3%), and Lululemon (76.3%). ASICS's 85.3% total op cost was achieved without heroic SG&A cuts — it came almost entirely from 610 bps of gross margin expansion. This frames the adidas improvement path: gross margin expansion to 53–55% range (equivalent to ASICS's FY2024 level) would be worth ~€500–1,000M of EBIT at current scale. This is more achievable than cutting 8pp of SG&A, which would require restructuring the athlete rights portfolio.

**Hypothesis 3: adidas gross margin recovery is real and durable — but the next 2pp will be harder**
[OBSERVABLE — margin data; INFERENCE — difficulty of next step]
> Moving from 47.3% (2022) to 51.0% (2025) was driven by Yeezy clearance completion, channel rebalancing, and full-price discipline. Moving from 51% to 53% requires: (a) meaningful DTC mix increase, (b) premium product mix shift, or (c) reduction in wholesale markdown intensity. ASICS precedent shows it is achievable but took 3 years of consistent execution.

**Hypothesis 4: adidas e-commerce is structurally under-monetized vs. peers**
[OBSERVABLE — 18% vs. peers at 25–40%; INFERENCE — cause is operational not purely cyclical]
> At 18% e-commerce penetration vs. Deckers (25%), Lululemon (35–40%), and PUMA growing e-com at +21%, adidas's digital channel is not capturing its share of the market shift online. Partly the Yeezy distortion (now resolved), but also a loyalty/personalization capability gap. Discovery question: what is adidas's repeat purchase rate through owned digital channels vs. peers? What is the AI/data investment level in personalization?

**Hypothesis 5: TRANS4RM is the enabling investment that unlocks all other value**
[INFERENCE — systems dependency analysis]
> Every AI/analytics and automation hypothesis depends on data quality and process standardization. Without a unified data backbone (SAP S/4HANA via TRANS4RM), demand forecasting AI, inventory optimization, and personalization all operate on fragmented data. The strategic question is not whether adidas should invest in AI — it is whether TRANS4RM is sufficiently complete to enable AI at scale.

**Hypothesis 6: Under Armour's operating cost trajectory is the cautionary benchmark for total cost structure failure**
[OBSERVABLE — UA data; INFERENCE — adidas analog]
> Under Armour demonstrates that gross margin improvement is necessary but insufficient. UA improved gross margin by +300 bps while total op cost % worsened to 103.6% GAAP — because SG&A% surged on declining revenue. adidas's 2028 challenge is the mirror: can it sustain SG&A leverage as revenue growth eventually moderates? Discovery question: what proportion of adidas's current SG&A is fixed vs. variable — and what is the realistic floor if currency-neutral growth drops below 5%?

**Hypothesis 7: ASICS's gross margin trajectory is the closest credible template for adidas's next expansion phase**
[OBSERVABLE — ASICS data; INFERENCE — adidas application]
> ASICS expanded gross margin from 49.7% to 55.8% in three years through channel mix improvement and pricing discipline — a broadly comparable mechanism to what adidas is executing. adidas at 51.0% (FY2025) is approximately where ASICS was entering FY2023. If adidas replicates two-thirds of ASICS's expansion rate (+400 bps), adidas reaches ~55% gross margin by 2028 — worth ~€1.0B of incremental gross profit vs. current at stable revenue. The discovery question: what proportion of ASICS's expansion came from (a) pricing vs. (b) channel vs. (c) input costs — and which levers are replicable at adidas's scale and brand portfolio breadth?
