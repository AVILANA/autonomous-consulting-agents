## PEER SELECTION RATIONALE

TJX operates at the intersection of two competitive arenas: off-price discount retail (primarily US-centric) and global value fashion. The peer set spans both arenas because TJX's International segment (TK Maxx in UK, Germany, Austria, Netherlands, Poland, Ireland, and Australia) competes directly against European fast fashion operators, while the US core competes against Ross Stores and Burlington Stores for the same shoppers and the same vendor pipeline.

Peers are organized in three tiers:
- **Tier 1 — Direct Off-Price Comparables:** same business model, same shoppers, same vendor pipeline
- **Tier 2 — International Value/Fast Fashion Peers:** different model but compete for the same customer wallet; provide efficiency benchmarks and operational ceilings
- **Tier 3 — Adjacent/Contextual Peers:** partial overlap; useful for sub-segment comparisons

**Final peer count:** 7 companies + TJX. Geographic diversity confirmed: Americas (Ross, Burlington, Gap), Europe (Inditex, H&M, Next), Global (TJX, Inditex, H&M, Gap). Off-price peers are structurally US-centric — that is accurate to the industry, not a selection gap.

## TIER 1 — DIRECT OFF-PRICE COMPARABLES

### 1. Ross Stores (NASDAQ: ROST)

**Why included:** Nearest business model match in the global peer set. Ross Dress for Less and dd's DISCOUNTS operate on an identical opportunistic buying model — no forward factory orders, no stable SKU (Stock Keeping Unit) catalog, treasure hunt in-store experience, high inventory turns, no loyalty program, and near-zero e-commerce by design. Both fiscal years end in January/February. Ross is the primary operational benchmark.

**Business overlap:** Very high. Ross and TJX compete for the same value-oriented US shoppers, the same vendor excess and closeout inventory, and the same US strip mall and suburban real estate. Buyer talent pipelines overlap. When either company expands buying aggressively (as both are doing in the current tariff-driven excess inventory cycle), they are bidding against each other for the same deals.

**Revenue (most recent):** $21.1B (FY2025, ends Feb 1, 2025) — 2.9x smaller than TJX FY2025 ($56.4B) and 2.7x smaller than TJX FY2026 ($60.4B). [OBSERVABLE]

**Operating model similarity:** Very high. Structural differences are of degree, not kind.

**Comparability caveats:**
- US-only vs. TJX's 9-country footprint — International adds structural overhead not present in Ross
- Ross has zero e-commerce (deliberate); TJX has minimal (~1-3% estimated) e-commerce
- Simpler assortment: fewer SKUs, fewer categories than TJX; fewer banners (2 vs. TJX's 8)
- ~1,756 stores vs. TJX's 5,214 — scale and management complexity very different
- Ross CapEx: $1.1B guided for FY2026 explicitly including DC (Distribution Center) automation — will likely widen the technology gap with TJX if TJX does not respond
- Ross's higher operating margin at smaller scale (12.2% vs. TJX 11.9%) is the central analytical anomaly — see STANDOUT PEER flag below

### 2. Burlington Stores (NYSE: BURL)

**Why included:** Third member of the US off-price triad. Directly competes for shoppers and vendors. More important than its absolute margin would suggest: Burlington is making the most explicit and public commitment to DC automation of any off-price peer, providing the nearest benchmark for what a technology-upgraded off-price DC looks like.

**Business overlap:** High. Burlington Coat Factory (now Burlington) competes for value-oriented US shoppers across apparel and accessories. Historically more apparel-focused than TJX; fewer home goods and lifestyle categories. Same treasure hunt model.

**Revenue (most recent):** $10.635B (FY2025, ends Feb 1, 2025) — 5.3x smaller than TJX FY2026. [OBSERVABLE]

**Operating model similarity:** High with structural caveats. Burlington has historically operated at lower margins, reflecting a less mature buying network, narrower category mix, and smaller vendor relationships. Now in an aggressive reinvestment phase.

**Comparability caveats:**
- **Gross margin accounting difference (critical):** Burlington's reported gross margin (~43.3%) appears materially higher than TJX's (~31.0%) because Burlington excludes certain occupancy costs from COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) that TJX includes. Direct gross margin comparison requires adjustment. On an adjusted basis, Burlington's gross margin would be substantially lower. [INFERENCE — standard retail accounting difference, widely noted by analysts]
- Still recovering from COVID/inflation cycle; operating margin ~6-7% vs. TJX 11.9% — not yet a profitability benchmark
- Burlington's primary analytical value is as an automation investment benchmark: their new Georgia DC, explicitly described as "highly automated," opening 2026, is the most concrete DC innovation reference in the off-price segment
- Burlington CapEx: $950M guided FY2025 on $10.6B revenue = 8.9% CapEx intensity — among the highest in the peer set [OBSERVABLE]

## TIER 2 — INTERNATIONAL VALUE / FAST FASHION PEERS

### 3. Inditex / Zara (BME: ITX)

**Why included:** Global operational efficiency ceiling. The only company in the peer set at comparable revenue scale (~$42B) that demonstrates dramatically superior operating economics — 19.7% EBIT (Earnings Before Interest & Tax) margin vs. TJX's 11.9%. The model is different (full-price, design-led), but the technology investment profile makes Inditex the single most important reference point for what is achievable.

**Business overlap:** Low on business model; moderate on customer competition. Full-price, design-led fast fashion (Zara, Pull&Bear, Massimo Dutti, Bershka, Stradivarius, Oysho, Zara Home). Competes directly with TJX International (TK Maxx) in the UK, Germany, and Netherlands for the same fashion-conscious 18-45 demographic. In those markets, TK Maxx and Zara are in the same shopping centers competing for the same wallet.

**Revenue (most recent):** €38,612M (~$42B USD, FY2024, ends Jan 31, 2025). [OBSERVABLE; USD conversion approximate at 1.09 USD/EUR]

**Operating model similarity:** Low. Inditex owns its design-to-shelf cycle with ~2-3 week lead times for trending items; TJX buys excess inventory after the fact, often months after the fashion cycle has peaked. Inditex's RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) deployment across all stores and all merchandise, integrated supply chain, and real-time sell-through data represent the current standard of operational excellence in global retail.

**Comparability caveats:**
- Full-price vs. off-price: structural gross margin difference (57.8% vs. 31.0%) reflects business model, not operational gap. Do not attempt direct gross margin comparison.
- Inditex controls production design; TJX does not. No direct product or sourcing comparison possible.
- Inditex e-commerce ~30% of sales (~€12B online); TJX ~1-3%. Not an apples-to-apples gap — TJX's model deliberately suppresses e-commerce.
- EUR-denominated; all USD conversions approximate.
- The 7.8pp operating margin gap (TJX 11.9% vs. Inditex 19.7%) reflects partly model difference, partly operational technology investment. At TJX's $60.4B scale, every 100bp of operating margin improvement = ~$604M in operating income.

**Key use in analysis:** Structural efficiency ceiling and long-range ambition benchmark. If TJX were to close even half the operating margin gap to Inditex through operational technology, the value would be ~$2.4B in additional annual operating income.

### 4. H&M Group (Nasdaq Stockholm: HM-B)

**Why included:** European value fashion leader with significant geographic overlap with TJX International. H&M operates in all of TJX's European markets (UK, Germany, Netherlands, Austria, Poland) and provides benchmarks for European market operating economics, e-commerce penetration, and customer data infrastructure.

**Business overlap:** Moderate. H&M targets the same value-seeking shopper in Europe. Both TK Maxx and H&M compete for UK and German mall real estate and the same 18-35 shopper demographic. H&M's 170M+ member loyalty program provides a direct contrast to TJX's zero-loyalty model — H&M knows exactly who is buying what; TJX does not.

**Revenue (most recent):** SEK 228,285M (~$21.5B USD, FY2025, ends Nov 30, 2025). Note: revenue declining slightly in SEK terms — partially FX (Foreign Exchange) effects, partially competitive pressure from Zara and Shein. [OBSERVABLE in SEK; USD conversion approximate at 0.094 USD/SEK]

**Operating model similarity:** Low. H&M designs and produces its own merchandise; runs a global loyalty program with 170M+ members that feeds demand forecasting; invests heavily in e-commerce (~25-30% of sales). The contrast with TJX's data-free, physical-only model is intentional and analytically important.

**Comparability caveats:**
- Swedish company, SEK-denominated. All USD conversions are approximate.
- Revenue declining in local currency — H&M is under competitive pressure, making it a cautionary benchmark rather than an aspirational one.
- Operating margin 8.1% — below TJX 11.9%, which is counterintuitive (full-price model with higher gross margins but lower net operating efficiency). Reflects high fixed-cost store estate and SG&A (Selling, General & Administrative) overhead.
- H&M's loyalty data is analytically valuable as a contrast: 170M+ members means demand signals are continuous and granular. TJX has no equivalent.

### 5. Next plc (LON: NXT)

**Why included:** Best-in-class case study for a value-oriented retailer that built a high-margin digital business without abandoning physical retail. NEXT Total Platform demonstrates that a branded retailer can run ~50%+ of sales online while sustaining ~17% net profit margin. Relevant to the TJX International e-commerce opportunity and to the question of what digital maturity could look like for an off-price model.

**Business overlap:** Low-moderate. UK-based; does not compete directly with TJX in the US. In the UK, NEXT and TK Maxx compete for the same mid-market shopper. NEXT's LABEL platform (third-party brand e-commerce fulfillment) is conceptually adjacent to a model TJX could build if it chose to enable brand partners to sell via a TJX digital platform — a model TJX has not pursued.

**Revenue (most recent):** £6.12B (~$7.7B USD, FY2025, ends Jan 25, 2025) — smallest peer in the set by revenue. [OBSERVABLE; USD conversion approximate at 1.26 USD/GBP]

**Operating model similarity:** Low-moderate. NEXT operates primarily at full price but with a strong value orientation in its product mix. Its e-commerce penetration (~50%+ of sales) and digital fulfillment model are architecturally different from TJX's physical-first, zero-loyalty model.

**Comparability caveats:**
- Net profit margin of ~17.3% is net margin, not operating margin — comparison to TJX's 11.9% operating margin is directional, not precise. Reported net vs. operating gap means NEXT's operating margin is likely 19-21% — even further above TJX.
- UK-only physical footprint (~500 stores); very different real estate economics from TJX's 5,214-store global estate.
- GBP-denominated; USD conversion approximate.
- Smaller absolute scale ($7.7B vs. TJX $60.4B) limits KPI comparisons; useful primarily as a qualitative reference for digital model design.

## TIER 3 — ADJACENT / CONTEXTUAL PEERS

### 6. Gap Inc. (NYSE: GAP)

**Why included:** US multi-brand specialty retailer undergoing a dramatic margin turnaround. Old Navy (~$8B+ revenue, the largest Gap banner) competes directly with TJX's Marmaxx segment for value-oriented US shoppers. Gap's recovery trajectory (operating margin from ~1% in FY2023 to ~7% in FY2024) is a relevant reference for speed of margin improvement under effective management.

**Business overlap:** Low-moderate on model; moderate on customer. Full-price specialty, but Old Navy's value positioning overlaps with Marshalls and TJ Maxx demographically. Gap's e-commerce at ~35% of sales provides a reference for where a traditional US retailer has moved digitally over the past decade.

**Revenue (most recent):** ~$15,270M (FY2024, ends Feb 1, 2025). [OBSERVABLE]

**Operating model similarity:** Low. Gap designs and sources its own merchandise; runs brand-specific stores; has a growing loyalty program. TJX buys third-party excess inventory with no design function. Included primarily for US market competitive context and e-commerce penetration reference.

**Comparability caveats:**
- Different COGS structure: Gap's COGS excludes DC and distribution costs that TJX includes — direct gross margin comparison requires adjustment.
- Operating margin ~7% (FY2024) is lower than TJX despite a similarly large US retail footprint — reflects legacy fixed-cost structure and ongoing restructuring.
- Gap's FY2023–FY2024 margin recovery (+600bp) is useful as a "speed of change" reference, not as a steady-state benchmark.

### 7. Nordstrom / Nordstrom Rack (NYSE: JWN)

**Why included:** Nordstrom Rack's off-price positioning overlaps directly with TJX's Marmaxx segment for premium-brand discount shoppers. Rack is the faster-growing segment of JWN and has e-commerce capability that TJX's Marshalls banner largely lacks — relevant to the off-price digital gap.

**Business overlap:** High for Rack segment; low for full-line Nordstrom. Nordstrom Rack and TJ Maxx/Marshalls target overlapping shopper segments — brand-conscious consumers seeking discounts on recognized labels. In premium-adjacent markets, Rack competes for the same high-value customer.

**Revenue (most recent):** ~$14.7B total JWN (FY2024, ends Feb 1, 2025). Nordstrom Rack revenues not separately disclosed. [OBSERVABLE — total JWN only]

**Operating model similarity:** Low at company level; high for Rack segment specifically. JWN as a whole includes full-price department stores. Rack operates more like TJX than JWN's full-line stores do. However, numeric comparisons are limited by the absence of Rack-level disclosures.

**Comparability caveats:**
- JWN does not separate Rack segment financials — no segment-level P&L comparison possible from public data.
- Total JWN operating margin ~2-3% estimated [ESTIMATED] is low relative to TJX — reflects full-price department store overhead dragging the blended figure.
- Nordstrom Rack's e-commerce is relevant qualitatively (Rack has it; Marshalls largely doesn't) but cannot be quantified from public sources.
- Included for qualitative e-commerce reference and premium off-price competitive context, not for margin benchmarking.

## STANDOUT PEER FLAGS

### STANDOUT PEER #1: Ross Stores — OPERATIONAL BENCHMARK

Ross Stores operates at **12.2% operating margin (FY2025)** on $21.1B in revenue — **higher than TJX's 11.9% (FY2026)** despite being 2.7x smaller. In standard economics of scale, a larger operation with more purchasing power and more fixed-cost leverage should produce a higher margin than a smaller one running the same model. TJX does not. This is the central anomaly in the peer comparison.

Three possible explanations:
1. **International drag:** TJX International at 7.3% margin dilutes the consolidated average. Stripping International, Marmaxx runs at 14.4% — far above Ross. If so, the issue is not operational inefficiency in the US; it is the underperforming International segment.
2. **Organizational complexity:** Running 8 banners across 9 countries introduces overhead (legal, HR, compliance, multi-currency treasury) that Ross does not carry. This is structural and only partially addressable.
3. **Scale not yet captured in DC operations:** Ross is investing $1.1B in CapEx (FY2026) including DC automation. If Ross closes its DC efficiency gap with TJX at the same time it becomes more automated, the margin differential could widen further.

**Analytical implication:** At TJX's $60.4B revenue base, each 100bp of operating margin improvement = **~$604M in additional operating income**. The Ross gap (30bp on reported margin; ~270bp if comparing Marmaxx to Ross directly to Ross's 12.2%) is the most commercially defensible quantification of the operational efficiency opportunity. **Make this comparison central to DC automation and buyer intelligence provocations.**

---

### STANDOUT PEER #2: Inditex — STRUCTURAL CEILING

Inditex operates at **~19.7% EBIT margin on €38.6B revenue (~$42B)**. At TJX's comparable revenue scale, this represents an **8.0pp operating margin gap** — equivalent to ~**$4.8B in additional annual operating income at TJX's FY2026 revenue base**. Inditex achieves this through RFID inventory tracking across all stores and merchandise, integrated design-to-shelf supply chain, and real-time sell-through data feeding AI-assisted allocation and replenishment. The model is different (full-price vs. off-price), but the implication is clear: at $40–60B in retail revenue, operational technology investment generates operating margins that manual operations cannot match.

**Analytical implication:** The Inditex benchmark is not a direct comparison — it is a structural ceiling showing what technology-led operations can produce at scale. Use Inditex as the long-range ambition reference. Use Ross as the near-term operational benchmark (same model, quantifiable gap, no model adjustment required).
