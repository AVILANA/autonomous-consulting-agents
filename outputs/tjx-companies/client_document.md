## What is this?

This document is based entirely on public information — annual reports, quarterly earnings, regulatory filings, investor presentations, and market data. We have not spoken with your team. What follows is our outside view of where the operational gaps are, how large they may be, and where the conversation deserves to go.

Every claim is tagged: **[FACT]** for confirmed disclosures, **[INFERENCE — HIGH]** for direct logical conclusions from confirmed data, and **[INFERENCE — MODERATE]** for reasoned estimates where alternative explanations exist. In the scorecard, **[EST.]** marks figures derived through calculation or cross-source triangulation — use them directionally, not as precise benchmarks.

Abbreviations used: DC = Distribution Center, COGS = Cost of Goods Sold, EBIT = Earnings Before Interest and Tax, SG&A = Selling, General and Administrative expense, CapEx = Capital Expenditure, FY = Fiscal Year, pp = percentage points, bp = basis points (hundredths of a percentage point), FCF = Free Cash Flow, YoY = Year-on-Year.

## Section 1 — Five Provocations

### Provocation 1 — Distribution Center Operations

**Burlington automated its new DC. TJX may be designing three new ones without it — and retrofitting will cost $300M more.**

**Financial lever:** COGS Avoidance | **Operational lever:** Throughput / Process Efficiency

**Evidence:**

- [FACT] Three distribution centers are under active construction: New Jersey (1.3 million square feet), El Paso TX (1.6–2.0 million square feet), and Ohio ($170M confirmed investment). No public announcement of operational automation design or a warehouse management system has been found in any TJX press release, earnings call transcript, or regulatory filing for any of these three facilities.

- [FACT] Burlington Stores has publicly described its new Savannah, GA distribution center as "highly automated" and is building a second automated facility in California. Burlington's capital expenditure intensity is 8.9% of revenue — 3.7 times TJX's estimated 2.4%. Ross Stores committed $1.1B in FY2026 capital expenditure with explicit operational infrastructure components.

- [INFERENCE — MODERATE] Automation and operational control infrastructure designed into a distribution center during construction costs an estimated $30–60M per facility. The same capability added after a distribution center opens costs approximately 3–5 times more — an estimated $90–300M per facility. Across three facilities, the difference between designing it in now versus retrofitting later is approximately $180M–$720M, with $300M as a reasonable midpoint.

**Cost of inaction:** Approximately $150M–$300M per year in avoidable cost — the ongoing throughput efficiency gap between a manually operated distribution center and a peer-benchmark automated facility. On top of the annual gap: an estimated $180M–$720M one-time retrofit penalty if automation is not designed in during the current construction phase.

**Discovery question:** Are the operational design specifications for the New Jersey, El Paso, and Ohio distribution centers finalized — and what throughput-per-labor-hour target was modelled for each new facility against TJX's existing network?

### Provocation 2 — Buying Decision Quality

**Your buyers are navigating TJX's best market ever with no deal scoring — and may be leaving $200M per year behind.**

**Financial lever:** Gross Margin Improvement | **Operational lever:** Decision Cycle Compression

**Evidence:**

- [FACT] TJX has approximately 1,400 buyers managing opportunistic purchases from 21,000+ active vendor relationships across 100+ source countries. No analytical decision-support system for buyer deal evaluation has been publicly confirmed. CEO Herrman stated in March 2026 that TJX is "going after brands in a more aggressive manner than we have ever had before" — the highest deal flow in company history, driven by tariff-related inventory surplus from conventional retailers.

- [FACT] Your enterprise procurement system and your store allocation tool contain historical deal data: vendor identity, category, price paid per deal, and store-level sell-through rates by category. No confirmed analytical layer connects this existing data to a buyer dashboard at the point of committing to a purchase.

- [INFERENCE — MODERATE] A 50 basis point improvement in markon — the markup above cost at which TJX buys from vendors — on approximately $41.7B estimated Cost of Goods Sold = approximately $209M in incremental gross profit per year. Management explicitly cited higher markon as a driver of gross margin improvement in FY2024–FY2026. Ross Stores generates approximately $240K revenue per employee versus TJX's approximately $163K — a 47% productivity gap on a directly comparable off-price operating model. [FACT — observable from public filings]

**Cost of inaction:** Approximately $200M per year in gross profit not captured — the estimated 50bp markon improvement opportunity across $41.7B in annual buying decisions, during the best sourcing environment in company history.

**Discovery question:** What is the distribution of markon achieved across buyer categories — top quartile versus bottom quartile — and what is the current average deal evaluation cycle time from first vendor contact to purchase commitment?

### Provocation 3 — Store Labor Scheduling

**Your 309,000 store employees are scheduled around absences. Scheduling around actual demand may be worth $300M a year.**

**Financial lever:** OPEX Reduction | **Operational lever:** Planning Cycle Speed

**Evidence:**

- [FACT] TJX employs approximately 309,000 store associates — roughly 85% of 364,000 total employees. TJX is adding 146 net new stores in FY2027. Each new store adds incremental scheduling complexity without a corresponding improvement in scheduling intelligence. At 7,000 stores (the long-term target), the estate is 34% larger than today.

- [FACT] Your workforce management platform is confirmed as handling absence management — tracking leave and call-ins. The demand-based scheduling capability, which aligns staff hours to predicted store traffic and inbound delivery timing, has not been confirmed as deployed. Your building management system, centralized across all US stores since June 2024, captures occupancy and energy data that could serve as a real-time traffic proxy for scheduling. No confirmed connection between these two systems has been found.

- [INFERENCE — MODERATE] At an estimated $5B–$7B in annual store labor cost, a 5% scheduling efficiency improvement — the industry benchmark for demand-based scheduling at comparable-scale retailers — represents approximately $250M–$350M per year. Gap Inc. improved its operating margin by +600bp in two years (FY2022 to FY2024) through systematic store operational discipline including labor optimization — a peer proof point that structured store-level improvements compound quickly. [FACT — Gap Inc. public filings]

**Cost of inaction:** Approximately $300M per year in scheduling inefficiency — the estimated midpoint of the range above, compounding with each of the 146 new stores added annually.

**Discovery question:** Is TJX's store labor scheduling currently driven by absence coverage or by predicted demand — and how is labor cost per store-hour tracked against plan across the 5,214-store estate?

### Provocation 4 — International Operations

**TJX International earns half Marmaxx's margin. At $8B revenue, the gap is worth $570M a year — and growing.**

**Financial levers:** OPEX Reduction + Working Capital Release | **Operational lever:** Response Latency

**Evidence:**

- [FACT] TJX International (TK Maxx UK, Germany, Austria, Netherlands, Poland, Ireland, Australia) reported 7.3% operating margin in constant currency for FY2026. Marmaxx (TJ Maxx + Marshalls + Sierra, US) reported 14.4% operating margin for the same period — a 710bp gap. At $8.0B International revenue (FY2026), the full gap represents approximately $568M in foregone annual operating income relative to Marmaxx-equivalent margins. New market entries confirmed for FY2027 onward — Spain (5 stores), Mexico (joint venture with Grupo Axo), and the Middle East (35% stake in Brands for Less) — will add International revenue volume before operational improvements take hold.

- [FACT] TJX has operated TK Maxx in the United Kingdom since 1994 — over 30 years — without publicly closing the operating margin gap to Marmaxx. No shared buying analytics, shared distribution center efficiency standards, or cross-segment operational intelligence program connecting Marmaxx and TK Maxx operations has been confirmed in any public filing or earnings disclosure.

- [INFERENCE — HIGH] At $8.0B International revenue (FY2026), a 200bp improvement in International operating margin = approximately $160M in additional annual operating income. A 300bp improvement = approximately $240M. Inditex operates across the same European markets (UK, Germany, Netherlands) at 19.7% EBIT margin (FY2024) — demonstrating that European retail structural costs alone do not account for TJX International's margin level. Gap Inc. improved operating margin by +600bp in two years through structured operational action — confirming the pace of improvement possible with systematic effort.

**Cost of inaction:** Approximately $160M per year in foregone operating income — the conservative estimate of a 200bp addressable margin improvement on $8.0B International revenue. As International revenue grows toward $12B+ with Spain, Mexico, and the Middle East, the annual cost of inaction grows proportionally.

**Discovery question:** What specific mechanism and timeline does TJX have to close the operating margin gap between TJX International and Marmaxx — and which cost categories (buying efficiency, distribution center operations, store labor, occupancy) account for the largest share of the 710bp difference?

### Provocation 5 — The Margin Thesis

**DC design, buying intelligence, labor, and International margin — four gaps worth $600M+ that could move TJX from 11.9% to 13%+.**

**Financial lever:** Operating Margin Expansion | **Operational lever:** Production-to-Shelf Velocity

**Evidence:**

| Gap | Mechanism | Conservative annual EBIT | High annual EBIT |

| Distribution center operations | 15% throughput improvement reducing embedded labor costs | $150M | $300M |
| Buying decision quality | 25–50bp markon improvement across ~$41.7B annual buying | $104M | $209M |
| Store labor scheduling | 5% efficiency improvement on estimated $5B–$7B store labor | $250M | $350M |
| International margin | 200–300bp improvement on $8.0B International revenue | $160M | $240M |

- [FACT] At FY2026 revenue of $60.4B, $664M in additional operating income = +110bp in operating margin — from 11.9% to approximately 13.0%. The 13%+ target is not hypothetical: Marmaxx already operates at 14.4% (FY2026). The gap between consolidated TJX (11.9%) and Marmaxx alone (14.4%) is primarily driven by International (7.3%) and multi-banner overhead. Closing half the International gap and improving US operations through the mechanisms in Provocations 1–3 moves the consolidated result toward 13%+ without requiring any improvement beyond what is already demonstrated within TJX's own business.

- [INFERENCE — MODERATE] At current share count and effective tax rate, $664M in additional operating income = approximately $0.45–$0.50 in additional adjusted earnings per share (EPS) — connecting directly to TJX's stated capital return and dividend growth commitments.

**Cost of inaction:** Approximately $664M per year at the conservative end — the combined annual value of four operational gaps, each with a confirmed public data foundation, and each with a Phase One validation mechanism that converts directional estimates into confirmed baselines.

**Discovery question:** What is TJX's internal operating margin target for FY2028–FY2030 — and has any internal baseline been established for the four areas above against which external findings could be measured?

## Closing

TJX is not a company that needs fixing. It is the world's most successful off-price retailer — $60.4B in revenue, 11.9% operating margin, $4.92B in free cash flow, record results across the board. The question is not whether the model works. It does. The question is whether it scales to 7,000 stores at the same economics.

At 5,214 stores, the model produces outstanding results with a relatively lean operational intelligence layer. At 7,000 stores — 34% more volume, 34% more distribution complexity, 34% more scheduling load — the same approach may not sustain 12%+ operating margins. Three distribution centers are being designed right now. A buying environment that CEO Herrman called "the best we have ever had" is in full swing today. International is at $8B and growing.

The right moment to close operational gaps is not during a crisis. It is during record performance, when the cash flow funds it and the organizational confidence supports it.

We would like 90 minutes to walk through the five observations, hear where we are right and where we are wrong, and decide whether Phase One is worth doing.
