## SECTION 6 — WHERE TJX OUTPERFORMS

**1. Absolute revenue scale and consistency [OBSERVABLE]**
$60.4B (FY2026). Largest off-price retailer globally by revenue — 2.9x Ross, 5.7x Burlington. Six consecutive years of revenue growth since COVID. CAGR ~6% (FY2022–FY2026). No peer in the off-price tier matches this combination of scale and consistency.

**2. Gross margin expansion velocity [OBSERVABLE]**
TJX gross margin expanded from 27.6% (FY2023) to 31.0% (FY2026) = +340bp in 3 years. This is the fastest gross margin expansion among off-price peers (Ross expanded +150bp over the same period; Burlington's numbers are not comparable due to accounting difference). Reflects improved buying efficiency, lower markdowns, and lower shrink. [OBSERVABLE]

**3. Free cash flow quality [OBSERVABLE]**
$4.92B FCF (FY2026) on $60.4B revenue = 8.1% FCF margin. No other peer in the off-price tier generates remotely comparable absolute FCF. Inditex (~12% FCF margin) and Next (~10% FCF margin) are structurally superior on FCF yield, but at smaller absolute volumes. TJX's $4.9B FCF funds both the expansion program ($2.2B CapEx) and capital returns ($2.5B–$2.75B buybacks) simultaneously — a sign of genuine cash generation, not leveraged engineering. [OBSERVABLE]

**4. Geographic diversification [OBSERVABLE]**
9 countries vs. US-only peers (Ross, Burlington). TJX International is margin-dilutive today (7.3%), but represents optionality that pure US peers do not have. Europe and Australia are underpenetrated relative to the US off-price model's maturity.

**5. Vendor network depth and tariff-cycle positioning [OBSERVABLE]**
~21,000 active vendor relationships across 100+ source countries. No peer discloses vendor relationships at this depth. In a tariff-driven excess inventory cycle (which the CEO described as "the best buying environment we've ever had" in March 2026), TJX's vendor breadth is a structural moat. [OBSERVABLE]

**6. Balance sheet strength [OBSERVABLE]**
$6.2B cash vs. $2.9B long-term debt = net cash positive. Debt/Equity ~0.34x. No financial risk constraining the expansion agenda.

## SECTION 7 — WHERE TJX LAGS

**1. Operating margin vs. Ross Stores: −30bp gap at 2.9x the scale [OBSERVABLE]**
Ross operates at 12.2% operating margin on $21.1B revenue. TJX operates at 11.9% on $60.4B. At scale, unit economics should improve — that's the fundamental logic of large-scale retail. TJX does not show this advantage vs. its most direct peer. Three contributing factors: (a) International segment at 7.3% drags the consolidated average — Marmaxx alone runs at 14.4%, far above Ross; (b) multi-banner organizational overhead (8 banners, 9 countries) that Ross does not carry; (c) DC network investment lag vs. Ross's $1.1B automation-heavy CapEx. [OBSERVABLE — numbers; INFERENCE — HIGH — implied implication]

**Quantification:** At TJX's $60.4B revenue base, each 100bp of operating margin improvement = **~$604M in additional annual operating income**. The Ross gap (30bp on consolidated comparison; ~220bp on Marmaxx-to-Ross comparison) represents a value pool of $180M–$1.3B depending on scope.

**2. E-commerce penetration: ~1–3% vs. 25–50% for fashion peers [ESTIMATED for TJX; OBSERVABLE for peers]**
TJX does not disclose e-commerce. The external estimate of ~$1.6B in online sales (Digital Commerce 360, 2024) implies ~1-3% of total revenue. Inditex: ~30% of €38.6B = ~€12B online. H&M: ~25-30% of ~$21.5B. Gap: ~35% of ~$15.3B. This is the widest capability gap in the peer set. Partly strategic (protecting the treasure hunt model), partly structural (no loyalty, no CRM at consumer level, no same-day delivery infrastructure). The strategic logic is sound for the US core. For International (UK, Germany), where Amazon and Zara have trained consumers to expect digital-first service, it may be eroding TJX's share of wallet. [INFERENCE — MODERATE]

**3. DC automation: no confirmed investment vs. explicit peer commitments [OBSERVABLE — absence]**
Burlington's new Georgia DC is "highly automated" (company description). Ross has committed $1.1B CapEx for FY2026 explicitly including DC automation. Neither TJX's FY2026 10-K nor any earnings call since 2024 has disclosed a DC automation program. TJX's FY2027 CapEx of $2.2B–$2.3B covers stores, remodels, DC expansion, and infrastructure — but no automation commitment has been made public. This is an absence-of-evidence finding, not a confirmed gap. However, given 21,000+ vendors shipping non-standard lot sizes with no stable SKU catalog, TJX's DCs handle arguably the most complex receiving challenge in the peer set — making automation the highest-value opportunity and the most glaring absence.

**4. Revenue per employee: ~$163K vs. ~$240K for Ross and Inditex [ESTIMATED]**
TJX generates ~$163K of revenue per employee (FY2026 estimate). Ross generates ~$240K on an estimated 88,000 employees — 47% more revenue per head. Inditex generates ~$239K. The TJX labor model is inherently more intensive: multiple deliveries per week per store, no stable SKU catalog requiring more handling per unit, and a 5,214-store estate with ~85% of employees in stores. Some of this gap is structural; some is addressable through AI-assisted scheduling, DC automation, and store operations optimization. [ESTIMATED]

**5. International segment margin: 7.3% vs. Marmaxx 14.4% [OBSERVABLE]**
The 710bp (basis point) gap between TJX's best-performing segment and its International segment is the largest internal performance gap in the business. At $8.0B International revenue (FY2026), every 100bp of margin improvement = ~$80M. Closing half the gap = ~$285M in additional operating income. No peer equivalent exists because Ross and Burlington are US-only — this is a TJX-specific structural challenge.

**6. Customer data infrastructure: none vs. 170M members (H&M), 50M+ members (Gap) [OBSERVABLE]**
No loyalty program. No consumer CRM. No disclosed customer data analytics program. H&M's 170M+ loyalty members feed demand forecasting algorithms. Gap's 50M+ rewards members provide purchase history at SKU level. TJX makes buying decisions for 21,000+ vendor relationships based on buyer intuition and category trend data — with no individual purchase behavior data to anchor it. This is partly strategic but creates a growing information asymmetry vs. peers as those programs compound. [FACT for the gap; INFERENCE — MODERATE for the implication]

## SECTION 10 — KEY BENCHMARK CONCLUSIONS

### Finding 1 — TJX scale advantage is partially captured [OBSERVABLE + INFERENCE — HIGH]
At $60.4B, TJX should generate structurally higher operating margins than Ross at $21.1B. It does not on a consolidated basis (11.9% vs. 12.2%). The Marmaxx segment (14.4%) does outperform Ross — the consolidated underperformance is driven by International (7.3%). This means the US business is already demonstrating scale leverage. The value capture opportunity lies in (a) improving International and (b) ensuring that the US DC/buying infrastructure is upgraded fast enough to maintain the Marmaxx margin advantage as volume grows 7% per year.

### Finding 2 — The DC automation gap is the most commercially defensible near-term opportunity [OBSERVABLE — absence + INFERENCE — HIGH]
Burlington (8.9% CapEx/revenue) and Ross (5.2%) are investing in DC automation at rates 2-4x TJX's current CapEx intensity (2.4%). Both have explicitly described automation programs. TJX has not. Given TJX's 21,000+ vendors shipping non-standard lot sizes with no stable SKU catalog, the receiving and processing complexity per unit is uniquely high. The automation opportunity is correspondingly large. Burlington's "highly automated" Georgia DC opening in 2026 will be the first proof point that off-price DC automation at scale is viable — making this the right moment for TJX to close the commitment gap.

### Finding 3 — The e-commerce gap is strategic, not operational — but it creates a customer data problem [FACT + INFERENCE — MODERATE]
TJX's ~1-3% e-commerce is a deliberate strategic choice to protect the treasure hunt experience. This is a coherent strategy for the US where the model is fully established. However, the consequence is zero customer data — no purchase history, no loyalty data, no demand signal from individual consumers. H&M's 170M member loyalty program feeds demand forecasting algorithms. Gap's 50M+ rewards program does the same. TJX's 1,400 buyers are making purchasing decisions without this individual-level signal — relying on aggregate category data and market intuition instead. At 5,214 stores and 7,000 target stores, the scale of this information gap is growing.

### Finding 4 — The Inditex ceiling is directionally correct but requires model adjustment [OBSERVABLE — Inditex; INFERENCE — MODERATE — TJX implication]
Inditex's 19.7% EBIT margin on ~$42B is the structural ceiling for what technology-led operations can produce at TJX's revenue scale. The 7.8pp gap is not all addressable (model difference accounts for ~4-5pp of gross margin difference), but the operating model discipline — RFID, real-time sell-through, AI-assisted replenishment — creates roughly 3-4pp of operating efficiency that is model-agnostic. At TJX's revenue base, 3pp = ~$1.8B in annual operating income. This is the long-range strategic ambition for an operational technology program.
